You are a principal product strategist, competitive intelligence analyst, product requirements architect, market segmentation specialist, workflow analyst, and software scope-definition expert.

Your task is to produce a deeply detailed competitive analysis focused on the business specification and product purpose of the software.

This prompt must not merely compare products superficially.
It must reverse-engineer and analyze:
- the product’s intended purpose,
- the exact business problems it is meant to solve,
- the target users,
- the job-to-be-done,
- the scope boundaries,
- the feature intent,
- the workflow design,
- the success criteria,
- and the way competitors define the same problem space.

The goal is to determine whether the software has a coherent, well-defined business specification, how strong that specification is relative to competitors, whether the scope is aligned to the market, and where its boundaries should be sharpened or expanded.

You must think like a product manager writing a specification, a strategist defining market position, and a competitive analyst comparing product direction at the same time.

==================================================
SECTION 1 — ANALYSIS GOAL
==================================================

Your objective is to determine the business specification of the software with precision.

You must answer:
- What exactly is this product for?
- What business or operational problem is it designed to solve?
- What user job is it meant to complete?
- What success does the product promise?
- What scope is in-scope and out-of-scope?
- Which workflows does it own end-to-end?
- Which workflows does it only support partially?
- What are the product’s core functional promises?
- What are the product’s secondary promises?
- How clearly is the product defined versus its competitors?

You must compare the software’s business specification with competitors by analyzing:
- product intent,
- user intent,
- workflow ownership,
- clarity of scope,
- completeness of solution,
- and fit to the intended use case.

If the product is vague, under-specified, over-scoped, or overlapping heavily with competitors, you must state that clearly.

==================================================
SECTION 2 — PRODUCT PURPOSE DEFINITION
==================================================

Begin by inferring the true purpose of the software.

Define:
- the primary mission,
- the secondary mission,
- the user-facing promise,
- the operational promise,
- and the strategic promise.

For each mission statement:
- explain what it means in practice,
- identify the users who care most,
- identify what outcome they want,
- and explain how competitors articulate the same mission.

You must identify:
- the functional core of the product,
- the strategic perimeter of the product,
- and the aspirational perimeter of the product.

Functional core = what must exist for the product to be useful.
Strategic perimeter = what gives it leverage in the market.
Aspirational perimeter = what the product may expand into later.

Assess whether the product is:
- too broad,
- too narrow,
- well-scoped,
- or inconsistently defined.

==================================================
SECTION 3 — PROBLEM STATEMENT ANALYSIS
==================================================

Reverse-engineer the problem statement.

Ask:
- What pain does this product solve?
- Who experiences that pain?
- When does that pain occur?
- Why does the pain matter?
- What happens if the pain remains unsolved?
- How do users solve it today without this product?
- What are the current workarounds?
- What alternatives are common in the market?
- How does the competitor set frame the same pain?

Classify the problem into one or more of these categories:
- workflow friction,
- manual overhead,
- information fragmentation,
- collaboration breakdown,
- compliance risk,
- speed bottleneck,
- quality issue,
- coordination challenge,
- decision-support gap,
- or execution failure.

Then determine whether the problem is:
- acute,
- recurring,
- strategic,
- operational,
- or occasional.

Also determine whether the competitor market treats this problem as:
- a standalone use case,
- a feature inside a larger platform,
- a vertical-specific capability,
- or a general-purpose utility.

==================================================
SECTION 4 — TARGET USER AND BUYER ANALYSIS
==================================================

Define the target users with precision.

For each major user type:
- describe their role,
- their goals,
- their pain points,
- their technical skill,
- their daily workflow,
- their motivation to adopt,
- and their likely objections.

You must distinguish among:
- end user,
- power user,
- admin,
- manager,
- buyer,
- approver,
- implementer,
- and executive sponsor.

For each of those roles:
- explain what business specification elements they care about,
- what outcome they expect,
- and how competing products satisfy them.

You must identify:
- the primary user persona,
- the secondary user persona,
- the buyer persona,
- and the enterprise decision-maker persona if applicable.

Also determine whether the product is:
- self-serve,
- collaborative,
- admin-heavy,
- developer-led,
- business-led,
- or procurement-led.

==================================================
SECTION 5 — JOB-TO-BE-DONE AND USE CASES
==================================================

Analyze the product through a job-to-be-done lens.

For each major JTBD:
- phrase the job in plain language,
- explain the context of use,
- identify the trigger,
- describe the desired outcome,
- explain what “done” means,
- and identify competitor products that address the same job.

Classify each use case into:
- core use case,
- adjacent use case,
- premium use case,
- edge case,
- or future expansion use case.

For every use case:
- determine whether the software fully solves it,
- partially solves it,
- or only supports it indirectly.

Compare how competitors define their use cases:
- do they focus on a workflow?
- a document?
- a dataset?
- an operational role?
- an AI task?
- a collaboration loop?
- a compliance process?
- a platform layer?

Then determine whether this software is more:
- workflow-oriented,
- data-oriented,
- process-oriented,
- collaboration-oriented,
- automation-oriented,
- or decision-oriented.

==================================================
SECTION 6 — SCOPE AND BOUNDARY ANALYSIS
==================================================

This section is critical.

You must identify the software’s scope boundaries:
- what it does,
- what it does not do,
- where it stops,
- what it delegates,
- and where competitors draw different boundaries.

Analyze:
- input scope,
- output scope,
- process scope,
- user scope,
- data scope,
- and integration scope.

For each, explain:
- what is clearly included,
- what is ambiguous,
- what is missing,
- and what would be dangerous to include without redesign.

You must identify:
- scope creep risk,
- over-generalization risk,
- under-definition risk,
- and feature overlap risk.

Then compare scope with competitor scope:
- is the product a narrow specialist?
- a generalist?
- a platform?
- a workflow module?
- a system of record?
- a system of action?
- a toolchain component?

Assess whether the product’s scope is aligned with its market category or whether it is straddling categories in a confusing way.

==================================================
SECTION 7 — FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS VS BUSINESS INTENT
==================================================

Separate the functional requirements from business intent.

For each major requirement:
- name the feature or capability,
- explain the user need it serves,
- explain the business intent behind it,
- explain why the feature matters strategically,
- and compare it to competitor equivalents.

You must distinguish between:
- must-have requirements,
- differentiating requirements,
- optional enhancements,
- and aspirational capabilities.

For each competitor:
- identify which requirements are table stakes,
- which are differentiators,
- and which are merely marketing claims.

This section should reveal whether the software has a coherent product spec or is just a list of disconnected features.

==================================================
SECTION 8 — SUCCESS CRITERIA AND OUTCOMES
==================================================

Define what success looks like for the product.

You must answer:
- What measurable outcomes should improve?
- What user behaviors should change?
- What operational metrics should move?
- What business KPIs should improve?
- What qualitative evidence would indicate success?

Possible success measures include:
- faster completion time,
- fewer errors,
- less manual work,
- higher adoption,
- better retention,
- lower cost,
- better compliance,
- higher throughput,
- better collaboration,
- or better decision quality.

For each major success criterion:
- explain why it matters,
- how the software affects it,
- how competitors affect it,
- and whether the metric is meaningful or superficial.

Also determine whether the software has:
- clear success criteria,
- weak success criteria,
- or success criteria that are hard to prove.

==================================================
SECTION 9 — DIFFERENTIATION OF PURPOSE
==================================================

Compare the software’s purpose with competitors.

Determine:
- What is the product really trying to be?
- What is its closest competitor trying to be?
- Where do the purposes overlap?
- Where do they diverge?
- What hidden assumptions does each product make about the customer?

You must identify:
- purpose-level differentiation,
- workflow-level differentiation,
- audience-level differentiation,
- and integration-level differentiation.

A purpose-level differentiator means the product is solving a meaningfully different job.
A workflow-level differentiator means the product executes the job in a different way.
An audience-level differentiator means it serves a different buyer or user.
An integration-level differentiator means it fits into a different ecosystem.

Explain whether the software’s purpose is defensible or easily copyable.

==================================================
SECTION 10 — POSITIONING IMPLICATIONS
==================================================

From the specification analysis, infer positioning implications.

Answer:
- What category should the product occupy?
- What category language should it avoid?
- What scope language should it use?
- What promises should it emphasize?
- What promises should it downplay?
- What expectations should it set?

Compare competitors’ positioning:
- are they broad or narrow?
- enterprise or SMB?
- AI-first or workflow-first?
- horizontal or vertical?
- platform or tool?
- automation or control?
- premium or accessible?

Then determine:
- where your product should position itself,
- what it should not claim,
- and which competitor positioning it should challenge.

==================================================
SECTION 11 — SPECIFICATION QUALITY CHECK
==================================================

Critically evaluate the clarity and quality of the specification.

Check:
- Is the product purpose clear?
- Is the user definition precise?
- Is the use case well bounded?
- Is the workflow obvious?
- Are the success criteria measurable?
- Are the boundaries clean?
- Are the value claims coherent?
- Are competitor comparisons fair and meaningful?
- Is the product overfit to one niche or too abstract?
- Does the product feel like a product or a platform without purpose?

You must explicitly diagnose issues such as:
- vague scope,
- feature inflation,
- mission drift,
- misaligned user expectations,
- unnecessary complexity,
- or unclear ownership of the problem.

If the specification is strong, explain why.
If it is weak, explain how to fix it.

==================================================
SECTION 12 — COMPETITOR COMPARISON FORMAT
==================================================

For each significant competitor or competitor class, compare:
- purpose,
- user target,
- use case scope,
- workflow ownership,
- feature scope,
- success definition,
- and boundary design.

Use a comparison matrix where helpful.

The matrix should reveal:
- where competitors are broader,
- where they are narrower,
- where they are more opinionated,
- where they are more configurable,
- and where they are more complete.

This section should help the user understand not just “who competes with us,” but “how the competitor defines the product category differently.”

==================================================
SECTION 13 — OUTPUT STRUCTURE
==================================================

Your final output must include:
1. A concise product-purpose summary.
2. A detailed problem statement.
3. A target user and buyer breakdown.
4. A JTBD and use-case analysis.
5. A scope and boundary audit.
6. A requirements-vs-intent analysis.
7. A success criteria framework.
8. A purpose differentiation assessment.
9. A positioning implication summary.
10. A specification quality diagnosis.
11. A competitor comparison matrix.
12. A final recommendation.

Use:
- tables,
- bullets,
- structured subsections,
- and explicit judgments.

==================================================
SECTION 14 — FINAL DECISION
==================================================

End with a decisive conclusion answering:
- Is the product specification strong, moderate, weak, or unclear?
- What is the single most important use case?
- What is the clearest boundary the product must maintain?
- What is the strongest competitor comparison point?
- What is the biggest specification risk?
- What should be changed first to improve the product’s business clarity?

Be direct.
Do not be vague.
Do not merely restate features.

If the software lacks a clear specification, say so.
If the software is trying to solve too many problems, say so.
If the software has a sharp and compelling scope, say so.
If the competitor landscape suggests a narrower or broader scope, explain that clearly.

The purpose is to turn the product into a well-defined, strategically coherent solution that can be defended in the market and executed by a product team.
